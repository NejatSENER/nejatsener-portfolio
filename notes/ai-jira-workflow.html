Shipping AI into a Jira workflow, quietly

Using an LLM to break down user-reported issues into estimated tasks — and the guardrails that kept it from going off the rails.

Oct 2025 · 6 min read · Nejat Berk Şener

A product manager I worked with once told me, with zero affect, that she had "a pretty strong feeling most of my job is turning vague Slack messages into tickets with estimates." Every team has one of these people. They are load-bearing, and they are also tired. This is a note about trying to give them a second pair of hands.

The system I'm going to describe is small, internal, and unglamorous. It takes a free-text issue reported by a user (a support ticket, a Slack message, a row from an intake form), asks an LLM to break it down into candidate engineering tasks with rough effort estimates, and creates those tasks in Jira — pre-linked, pre-labeled, waiting for a human to approve. It is not replacing product management. It is replacing the first fifteen boring minutes of writing out what the person already meant.

The initial naive version (and why it was bad)

My first version was about two hundred lines. It took the issue text, stuffed it into a prompt along with "return a JSON array of Jira tasks," called OpenAI, parsed the response, and hit POST /rest/api/3/issue for each item.

It worked in the happy case. It was also terrible, in instructive ways:

  • Sometimes the LLM returned one task. Sometimes it returned twelve. The twelve were all plausible. The twelve were also all overlapping.
  • It occasionally hallucinated reasonable-looking Jira field values (priorities, labels) that didn't exist in our instance and quietly failed the create call.
  • Its effort estimates bore no relationship to how our team actually estimates, because it had never seen our estimates.
  • When the input was ambiguous, it confidently made something up rather than asking.

None of these were surprises. All of them needed to be designed around before this was a thing you'd let near a real backlog.

Guardrails, in the order I added them

1. A schema with teeth. I switched to OpenAI's structured outputs and defined a JSON schema that mirrored our Jira fields exactly — including an enum of valid priorities and a controlled list of component labels. The LLM can only emit shapes the schema allows. Most of the "hallucinated field" problem disappeared with one change.

2. A hard cap on task count. The prompt now says "produce at most five tasks; prefer fewer, larger tasks over more, smaller ones." The schema enforces maxItems: 5. If a problem really needs more than five sub-tasks, that's a human-planning conversation, not an LLM task-break conversation.

3. Few-shot examples from our own tickets. I picked 12 representative historical issues and their final Jira breakdowns, and included them in the system prompt. Estimates suddenly started landing in the range our team actually uses. "Small" stopped meaning "15 minutes" and started meaning "half a day." This was the single biggest quality jump.

4. Confidence gating and "ask instead." The schema includes a required confidence field per task (low, medium, high) and an optional clarifying_question at the top level. When overall confidence is low, the system posts a comment on the original issue with the clarifying question instead of creating tasks. This was the difference between "useful coworker" and "enthusiastic junior who won't stop."

5. Everything is a draft, not a ticket. Created tasks land in a Triage column, tagged ai-draft, assigned to nobody. A human has to promote them. This preserves the product manager's authority to reject, merge, or rewrite — which is the part of the job I was explicitly not trying to replace.

The part that surprised me

The quality of the AI output was not, in the end, the hardest part. The hardest part was trust — specifically, the reviewer's mental model of how much to trust the output on any given day.

Early on, people either rubber-stamped everything or rejected everything. Both failure modes were about the same underlying thing: they couldn't tell why a given draft had been generated the way it had.

The fix was boring and worked immediately: every AI-drafted task now includes, in the description, a one-line summary of what the LLM thought the original request was asking for, and which source sentence triggered each task. Reviewers stopped rubber-stamping. They also stopped rejecting reflexively. They started having opinions — "no, that's not what they meant, it's this" — and those edits became feedback data for the next iteration of the prompt.

Where I'd go next if I had more time

I'd wire the reviewer's edits back into the system as examples. Every "here's what the human actually shipped instead" is the highest-quality training signal you'll ever get; it just needs a low-friction way to land back into the prompt. I'd also watch estimate drift over months — my intuition is that LLM-estimated story points quietly creep downward if you don't calibrate against what actually got delivered.

The UI never got past prototype, which is honestly fine. The value of this system isn't the interface; it's that the backlog is twenty minutes closer to being actionable every morning.

I'm not an AI-skeptic and I'm not an AI-evangelist. I think LLMs are tools that occasionally solve problems cheaply and regularly create new problems expensively, and the craft is knowing which one is happening. Building this taught me a lot about that line. Tell me about yours.

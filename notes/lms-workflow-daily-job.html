Turning a manual LMS workflow into a daily job

What I learned automating training sync for 25,000+ users across a holding group — and why the scheduler was the easy part.

Mar 2026 · 7 min read · Nejat Berk Şener

The first time someone walked me through how training data moved between our HR system and our learning platform, they finished the explanation with a sentence I've been thinking about ever since: "and then Ayşe exports it to Excel every morning."

Ayşe — not her real name — had been doing this for three years. Every weekday at 9 AM she pulled a CSV from one system, ran a couple of pivot tables in Excel, saved it to a network share, and uploaded it to the LMS. About forty minutes of her morning, every morning, for a pipeline that served 25,000+ employees across seven group companies.

This is the kind of workflow that doesn't make it into architecture diagrams. It works. Nobody complains. And the day Ayşe goes on vacation, somebody panics.

The scheduler was the easy part

When I got assigned to automate this, my first instinct was the obvious one: write a nightly job. Hangfire in .NET, pull from the HR API, push to the LMS API, log the diff, done. I even had the first draft running in an afternoon.

That version would have been a disaster.

The problem wasn't the schedule — cron-style scheduling has been a solved problem since before I was born. The problem was everything around the schedule that Ayşe had been quietly doing in her head.

Things a human was doing that I hadn't noticed

Here's what I found once I sat with her for a morning and actually watched:

  • If a name had non-ASCII characters that the LMS export had mangled, she'd fix it by hand.
  • If someone had been reassigned between companies mid-quarter, she'd catch it because she recognized the name.
  • If a training completion came through with a date in the future (HR system timezone bug, turns out), she'd just… not upload that row.
  • If the LMS returned an error for an upload, she'd retry it. If it errored twice, she'd ping the LMS vendor. She had a list of who to ping.

None of this was written down. All of it was real work.

What the actual service looks like

The version that shipped — and has been running for a bit over a year now without needing anyone's morning — is built around three ideas:

One. The scheduled job doesn't do the work. It enqueues the work. Each user-sync and each training-record-sync is an independent unit that gets retried, logged, and inspected on its own. If the LMS API has a bad ten minutes at 3 AM, we don't lose the run — we just finish a bit later, and nobody notices.

Two. Everything Ayşe did in her head is now explicit. We have a named, versioned rule for Turkish character normalization. We have an inter-company transfer detector that matches on employee ID and flags confidence. We have a sanity-check layer that refuses to upload rows with impossible dates and writes them to a reviewer queue instead. When a new edge case shows up, we add a new rule; we don't patch the job.

Three. The reviewer queue is the most important part of the system. Automation isn't "replacing Ayşe." It's replacing the forty boring minutes and keeping the five interesting ones. Once a week, someone looks at the queue — maybe ten rows that need a human call — and the system learns from the resolutions.

What I'd tell the version of me who started this

A few things, in no particular order:

Write down every manual step you're replacing, and check off each one in the automated version. The checklist becomes your acceptance test. If a step isn't worth automating, at least make it visible — flag it, queue it, notify someone. Silent skipping is how trust in automated systems dies.

Don't run your first cron job at 3 AM. Run it at 10 AM for a week, in parallel with the human process, and diff the output. If the diffs are boring, you've earned the night shift.

The observability you need is not "did the job succeed." It's "does the output look like the output we expected." Record-count deltas, completion-rate deltas per company, a daily summary email that a human actually reads for the first month. Once the summary is boring, you can stop sending it.

And the part I keep relearning: the 100% in "100% automated" is always a bit of a lie, and that's fine. There will always be a reviewer queue. The goal isn't zero humans — it's zero humans doing the same boring thing twice.

Written from Istanbul, between two cups of çay. If you've built something similar — especially the part about teaching an automated system which errors are "normal" — I'd love to hear about it.
